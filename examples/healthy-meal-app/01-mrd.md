## Market problem
Working professionals want to eat healthily but lack time to plan, shop, and cook. Existing delivery apps optimize for speed and indulgence, not nutrition. Result: people default to unhealthy takeout or skip meals.

## Target market & segments
- **TAM:** online food delivery, ~$1T globally (illustrative).
- **SAM:** health-conscious urban professionals, 25–45, in major metros.
- **SOM (Y1):** 50k subscribers in 3 launch cities.

## Customer personas
| Persona | Pain point | Current workaround | Willingness to switch |
|---|---|---|---|
| Busy professional | No time to plan healthy meals | Random takeout / skipping | High |
| Fitness-focused | Hard to hit macros | Manual meal prep on weekends | Medium-high |

## Competitive landscape
| Competitor | Strengths | Weaknesses | Positioning |
|---|---|---|---|
| Generic delivery apps | Huge selection, fast | Not nutrition-focused | Convenience |
| Meal-kit services | Healthy, planned | Requires cooking | DIY healthy |

## Market trends & timing
Rising health awareness, normalized food delivery post-2020, and growth in dietary-preference diets (keto, vegan) create a gap for a *nutrition-first* delivery experience.

## Opportunity & high-level direction
A delivery app where meals are filtered by dietary preference, ordered in seconds, tracked live, and easily reordered — removing the planning burden entirely.

## Risks & assumptions
Demand assumes professionals will pay a premium for healthy convenience; competitive risk from incumbents adding filters. Data confidence: medium.
